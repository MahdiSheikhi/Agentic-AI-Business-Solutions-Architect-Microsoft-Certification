A · Why a maturity model at all

the scaling problem

1 · Pilot purgatory — why early wins stall

Many AI initiatives succeed as pilots but never become an enterprise operating model. The model exists to answer four questions every organization eventually asks.

1Agentic AI adds autonomous decision-making, multistep orchestration, and human–agent collaboration — which is why it needs a new enterprise operating model, not just new tools.
2The recurring failure mode: pilots succeed, scale stalls — initiatives stay isolated, siloed, and dependent on individuals.
3The model is deliberately holistic, not technology-only: strategy, process transformation, governance, value realization, architecture, operations, organizational readiness, and responsible AI.
4It is designed to be actionable — align leaders, architects, and delivery teams on priorities and focus investment where it matters most.

2 · Lineage and neighbors — where this model sits

It borrows a proven shape and plugs into the rest of Microsoft's adoption guidance.

PESibling: Platform Engineering Capability ModelMicrosoft applies the same CMM pattern to platform engineering — recognizing the shape earns easy exam points.
🛠Scope: every agent-building surfaceCovers Copilot experiences and agents built with Agent Builder in Microsoft 365 Copilot, Copilot Studio, and Microsoft Foundry — they participate in workflows and trigger actions, not just answer questions.
5Based on the Capability Maturity Model (CMM) — a widely used development model for assessing and enhancing organizational maturity.
6Aligns with the Agent Readiness Framework; a free agent readiness assessment exists online to score yourself.
7As adoption grows you must evolve multiple dimensions at once — strategy/UX, process + value measurement, governance/security/ops, technology + data access, culture + skills.
8Responsible AI and trust are embedded across all dimensions, not a sixth pillar on the side.

B · The model itself

5 levels × 5 pillars

3 · The five maturity levels — 100 → 500 staircase

Each capability pillar is graded on the same five-step ladder, from early experimentation to optimized enterprise-scale operation.

9Recite the ladder: 100 Initial → 200 Repeatable → 300 Defined → 400 Capable → 500 Efficient.
10100 Initial: unplanned, experimental; capabilities are inconsistent, siloed, and dependent on individuals rather than repeatable practices.
11200 Repeatable: teams can repeat certain activities, but approaches remain informal and uneven across the organization.
12300 Defined is the pivot: capabilities become formally defined, documented, and supported by governance, standards, and operating models — and initiatives align to business goals.
13400 Capable: agents sit inside enterprise planning and operations; process, governance, and technology support scaling and cross-team work. 500 Efficient: the agent-first enterprise, continuously improving on leadership, culture, and trust.
Don't confuse the ladders: this model's Levels 100–500 grade capability pillars for agent adoption. The CAF Plan phase's AI maturity Levels 1–4 (L1 ready-made Copilots … L4 custom model training) grade what an org can build. Same word "maturity," two different exam answers.

14The 200 → 300 jump is formalization: informal habits become documented standards, governance, and operating models.
15The 300 → 400 jump is integration: from having standards to agents embedded in enterprise planning and operations with automation and feedback loops.
16The 400 → 500 jump is culture: optimization becomes continuous and predictive, and AI-first thinking is the enterprise default.

5 · The five capability pillars — what gets graded

Maturity is assessed per pillar. Responsible AI isn't a pillar — it's the slab everything stands on.

17Name all five: AI strategy & experience · Business strategy · AI governance & security · Technology & data · Organization & culture.
18AI strategy & experience = aligning initiatives with business objectives, leadership priorities, long-term strategy, and UX goals.
19Business strategy = redesigning end-to-end processes for human–agent collaboration and measuring business impact / value realization.
20AI governance & security = guardrails, controls, oversight, operational management, and lifecycle governance for risk and compliance at scale.
21Technology & data = scalable, secure foundations, architectures, and data access patterns; Organization & culture = people, roles, incentives, ways of working.
22These five pillars are the same five capability drivers that the agent adoption patterns map their scale-breakers to — one framework, reused across the guidance.
Exam hook: when a question asks which dimension a symptom belongs to — "no champions or communities" → Organization & culture; "no reference architecture" → Technology & data; "no value measurement" → Business strategy; "no operational model" → AI governance & security; "no executive sponsor" → AI strategy & experience.

C · How to use the model

7 steps · radar · rules

7 · The seven-step method — planning tool, not scorecard

Microsoft's how-to article is explicit: use it for decisions, never as a one-time assessment.

1Assess current state by pillarGrade each of the five pillars independently against the 100–500 descriptions. Be evidence-based, not aspirational; score what is consistently true, not isolated wins.
2Never collapse to one scoreAdoption doesn't progress uniformly — a single average masks critical gaps that create risk later. Each pillar matures at its own pace.
3Visualize with a radar chartPlot the five pillar levels as a pentagon. The shape is more informative than the average — thin areas show where scaling breaks.
4Identify gaps & dependenciesReview progression opportunities: missing capabilities, invisible risks, and cross-pillar dependencies (e.g., scaling technology without matching operations or governance).
5Prioritize incrementallyPick one or two priority pillars and move each one level up, aligned to specific progression opportunities — momentum with managed risk.
6Use it as shared languageAlign business, IT, security, and leadership on what "good" looks like per stage; it moves conversations from opinion to evidence.
7Reassess regularlyMaturity changes as agents ship. Revisit periodically, fed by usage, outcomes, incidents, and telemetry — at high maturity, reassessment becomes continuous.
26Framing sentence to memorize: a practical planning and decision-making tool, not a one-time assessment or scorecard.
27Four stated uses: understand readiness · identify gaps that block scale · prioritize investments · guide safe progression toward more autonomous agents.
28Uneven profiles are expected — e.g., strong in security and governance while early in process redesign or organizational readiness. The model supports this.
29Worked examples of one-level moves: clearer intake, prioritization, and ownership lifts organizational readiness 200 → 300; consistent metrics and dashboards lift value realization 200 → 300.

8 · The radar profile — current state vs near-term target

The model's signature artifact. Solid = where you are; dashed = deliberate next investments. Notice the target raises only the weak pillars — not everything at once.

30The radar's job: give stakeholders a quick view of where to focus first — thin or recessed areas are where scaling breaks if you expand adoption anyway.
31Core message of the chart: you don't invest in every pillar at once — target the few constraints most likely to limit scale, trust, or value next.
32Overlay current vs near-term goal to show how targeted investments close critical gaps and reduce scaling risk.
33Microsoft's own sample profile: higher in strategy alignment and technology, lower in operations, organizational readiness, value realization, and responsible AI.
34Target shapes reflect deliberate choices, not raising all areas equally — near-term goals depend on your objectives and constraints.

9 · Progression rules — do this, not that

The how-to article is as clear about anti-patterns as it is about steps.

✔ Do

  • Grade on evidence — observable, consistently true behavior
  • Keep five separate pillar scores; expect an uneven profile
  • Move 1–2 pillars, one level at a time, tied to named progression opportunities
  • Watch dependencies between pillars before scaling one alone
  • Reassess on a rhythm, fed by telemetry, outcomes, and incidents
  • Use the levels as a shared vocabulary for leadership and roadmap talks

✘ Don't

  • Grade aspirationally or off one team's isolated success
  • Collapse the model into a single overall score — it masks critical gaps
  • Try to move every pillar forward at once
  • Scale technology while operations or governance lag behind
  • Treat the assessment as a one-time scorecard
  • Increase agent autonomy before the supporting capabilities exist
35The single-score trap is a likely exam distractor: the correct posture is per-pillar assessment because pillars mature at different paces.
36"What should the organization do first?" answers usually equal: fix the thinnest pillar one level, not leap to 500 or buy more technology.
37Autonomy gating: the model exists partly to answer "what capabilities do we need before increasing agent autonomy?" — capability first, autonomy second.
38At higher maturity, reassessment becomes continuous — maturity signals embed directly into planning, governance, and operational reviews.
