Requirement analysis

does this even need an agent?
In brief: good planning starts with three questions — what problem are we solving, what are the constraints, and does this genuinely need an agentic approach? Agents shine on variable, multi-step, tool-heavy work (contract review with risk flagging); rule-based, single-step, or pure-Q&A work belongs to flows and standard copilots. A four-step framework (goal → constraints → fit → recommendation) plus an explicit risk assessment (likelihood × impact × mitigation) makes a plan exam-grade.

1 · Agentic or not? — the fit sorter

Wrong exam answers apply agentic AI to problems a simple workflow already solves. Sort the scenario first.

✔ Strong agent candidates

  • Real variability + multi-step decisions — "evaluate this contract, flag risks, suggest approvals"
  • Ambiguous processes — supply-chain exception handling
  • Multiple tools / APIs in one workflow
  • High-compliance work run inside guardrails

✘ Not agentic — use the simpler tool

  • Rule-based: "gold customer → 15% discount" → Power Automate flow
  • Simple Q&A with no follow-up actions → standard copilot
  • Static single-step processes → traditional automation
1Three opening questions of every plan: what outcome? what constraints? does it need agents at all?
2Agentic test: multiple steps? tool/API calls? adapts when things go sideways? Three yeses = agent territory.

3 · Risk assessment — likelihood × impact × mitigation

Planning isn't just what to build; it's what could go wrong. The contract-review agent's own risk register:

Data stalenessMediumHigh — outdated regulationsautomated data refresh + alerts

4Rate every risk on likelihood and impact, then name a mitigation: guardrails, human review, monitoring, or a manual fallback.
5Context sets impact: a hallucination in an internal FAQ is low-impact; the same hallucination in a customer financial recommendation is high-impact.
🧠 Hook: in any "plan or recommend" scenario, include at least one risk + its mitigation — the exam rewards candidates who think about what can go wrong.

Data grounding readiness

hallucination insurance
In brief: grounding means giving the agent real context — RAG, connectors, knowledge bases — instead of letting it confidently invent answers. Before building anything, grade the data on three dimensions (quality, relevance, availability), run the readiness checklist (outdated data, silos, permissions, governance), and only then pick the tooling: Copilot Studio knowledge sources for simple grounding, Foundry for advanced RAG pipelines, Power Platform connectors for live enterprise data. Garbage in, garbage out is effectively a security issue.

The Cloud Adoption Framework for AI

pilot → organization-wide
In brief: CAF for AI is the roadmap from one pilot to a governed, organization-wide strategy: Strategy (pick high-impact use cases), Plan (data, skills, timeline), Ready (environments, security policies, ownership), Adopt (pilot then scale), Govern (continuous compliance with tools like Purview). The trap is treating it as a straight line — the phases run in parallel and loop: while use case #1 is in Adopt, use case #2 is already back in Strategy. A cross-functional AI Center of Excellence centralizes governance, and pilots are designed with scope, duration, metrics, go/no-go criteria, and a feedback loop.

6 · The CAF cycle — five phases, running in parallel

Iterative, not sequential. The arrow back from Adopt to Strategy is the exam's favorite detail.

10All five phases are ongoing, with activities in parallel — "CAF is iterative, not sequential" is the tested insight.
11The CoE exists because AI needs mutually-exclusive skill sets — no single person covers strategy, data, security, and change management alone.

7 · Anatomy of a well-designed pilot

"Let's try and see what happens" is not a pilot. These five elements are — defined before you start.

🎯Scopebounded: "password resets + account status for the NA support team" — not "automate all of customer service."
📅Duration6–8 weeks — long enough for meaningful data, short enough to keep urgency.
📊Success metricsresolution rate, CSAT, average handle time, escalation rate — defined up front.
🚦Go / no-go criteria"resolution > 70% and CSAT ≥ 4 → full rollout" — specific, agreed in advance.
🔁Feedback loopweekly team check-ins + automated post-interaction surveys feeding improvements.
12A structured pilot with clear success criteria beats "we'll deploy and monitor" every single time.
13If you don't define success before the pilot, you can't prove it afterward — metrics come first.

Build vs buy vs extend

the practical decision
In brief: build when the workflow is truly unique and you have deep expertise plus time and budget; buy when a vendor solution covers 80%+ out of the box; extend — start from a vendor base and customize prompts, connectors, and actions — is the most common enterprise answer. The decision reduces to two questions: how unique is the problem (usually less unique than people think) and how much expertise is on staff. Maintenance and ongoing ownership cost matter more than initial delivery.

ROI & total cost of ownership

prove it to finance
In brief: TCO is everything, not just tokens — development, inference, infrastructure, governance/operations, and change management, in roughly known proportions. ROI = (benefit − TCO) ÷ TCO. The worked example: an agent absorbing 40% of support tickets frees 20 FTEs ($1.4M) and cuts churn ($750K) against a $600K year-one cost — 258% ROI, break-even in three to four months. Exam answers include all cost buckets, realistic numbers, and phased rollout thinking.

Five buckets with typical shares — and the four levers that shrink them.

🔀Model routingstops premium pricing on simple queries — biggest inference lever.
♻️Reuse agents & promptsprompt libraries cut development cost — no rebuilding from scratch.
🛰️Purview + automated alertsshrink governance & operations cost.
🪜Phased pilotsreduce risk — never bet the organization on one big build.

Prompt engineering & prompt libraries

prompts as a strategic asset
In brief: the prompt is the agent's constitution — priorities, boundaries, and when to ask for help. Four techniques carry the exam: chain-of-thought, few-shot examples, role prompting, and guardrails. The organizational failure mode is every team writing subtly-different prompts from scratch; the fix is a centralized prompt library — categorized, performance-tracked, change-logged — governed with ownership, review before production, quarterly testing, and versioned templates pushed to all agents. The Center of Excellence owns it.

11 · Four techniques + the governed prompt library

What each technique buys you, and the library workflow that scales them across 15 agents instead of one.

🪜Chain of thoughtreason step-by-step before answering — improves reasoning, gives transparency.
🎯Few-shot examples2–3 concrete good answers — examples beat descriptions every time; ensures consistency.
🎭Role promptingtell the agent what role it plays — context changes behavior dramatically.
🛡️Guardrailsboundaries that prevent bad decisions — "never approve without legal review if IP rights are involved."
20The prompt is the agent's constitution — it defines priorities and when to ask for help vs decide alone.
21High-scoring phrase: "centralized prompt library with versioning, ownership, and governance."
22Cross-agent consistency: one escalation template (frustration, refunds over a threshold, legal interpretation → human) used by every agent.

Model routing & custom models

the economics of inference
In brief: not every task deserves the expensive model. Routing matches queries to models — lightweight for "my password isn't working," premium for a multi-cause enterprise regression — and Foundry can do it dynamically by evaluated complexity. Four criteria drive the rules: complexity, latency, cost targets, accuracy requirements. Custom models are the rare top rung of an escalation ladder (routing → prompting + grounding → fine-tuning), justified only by mission-critical domain accuracy, compliance isolation, or volume economics.

12 · Routing in one picture — and the four routing criteria

Same agent, two queries, two very different bills.

🧩Complexity — yes/no → cheap; open-ended analysis → premium
⏱️Latency — time-sensitive tasks can justify a faster model
💵Cost targets — build the per-query threshold into the rules
🎯Accuracy — safety-critical / compliance → always the best model
23Over thousands of calls, routing saves real money while keeping quality where it matters — a real policy balances all four criteria.

13 · The escalation ladder — and the three custom-model triggers

Most organizations never climb past rung two. Each trigger comes with the course's own numbers.

⚖️Mission-critical accuracylegal document analysis — a hallucination costs lawsuits; fine-tune on thousands of the firm's real contracts.
🏥Compliance isolationhealthcare data residency — fine-tuned model deployed in your own Azure subscription, full control.
📈Volume economics1M emails/yr: +2% accuracy saves $500K; $50K fine-tune pays back in ~3 months.
24Exam default: start with routing + advanced prompting, evaluate with metrics, escalate only on evidence of persistent gaps.
25Justify every model decision with metrics — accuracy gains, cost savings, compliance needs.

Data interoperability & architecture

from silos to a governed layer
In brief: data scattered across Dynamics, legacy databases, Zendesk, SharePoint, and SAP confuses agents into hallucination. Interoperability is the ongoing practice of making information flow consistently and securely: semantic organization (one shared vocabulary), governed access (permissions + audits), and near-real-time sync. Microsoft's kit: Purview catalogs everything with lineage, Dataverse holds the critical business entities, connectors and MCP reach the rest. The exam blueprint is a four-layer architecture, illustrated with a healthcare example.

14 · The four-layer data architecture

The pattern to reproduce on the exam whenever a scenario says "scattered and inconsistent data."

Healthcare example: EHR, claims, and billing stay put (L1); Purview maps them (L2); patient ID, insurance status, balance sync daily into Dataverse (L3); the front-desk agent sees insurance status but never medical records, "what's my copay?" hits Dataverse, "what tests did I have last year?" goes to the EHR via a secure connector (L4).

26Siloed data causes agent failures — inconsistent context is a hallucination generator, so interoperability is foundational, and ongoing.
27Semantic organization: "customer" and "order status" must mean the same thing, with the same values, in every system.
28Exam checklist: catalog with Purview → critical entities into Dataverse → connect the rest → lock down governance. Touch all four and you've nailed it.

Prebuilt agents & small language models

the simplest thing that works
In brief: you rarely build from scratch. Prebuilt copilots — Sales (deal workflows), Service (frontline support), Microsoft 365 (productivity) — are the fastest path to demonstrable value and ideal pilot vehicles. Where they run out (specialized industry workflows, deep custom integrations), small language models like Phi-3 step in: dramatically cheaper, private, fast enough for edge devices, and fine-tunable into a domain expert. The pragmatic hierarchy: prebuilt first, SLMs for the specialized edges, custom large models only as last resort.

🎧Copilot for Servicefrontline support — suggests solutions from knowledge articles, drafts responses, handles escalations. ROI = reduced handle time + higher resolution.

29Prebuilt agents are the fastest path to demonstrating value — which makes them the ideal pilot vehicle (and why "buy first" usually wins).
30Their limits: highly specialized industry workflows and deeply customized integrations — that's where you extend, or reach for SLMs.

16 · Truck vs scooter — when a small language model wins

GPT-4 is the truck; Phi-3 is the scooter. You don't need a truck to go get coffee.

💵Cost — dramatically cheaper per inference at millions of queries
🔐Privacy — fine-tune on proprietary data, deploy in your own Azure environment
⚡Latency — runs on edge devices: factory floor, retail store, no cloud round-trip
🎓Specialization — learns your terminology, processes & decision patterns

Two reference cases: factory sensors needing <100 ms anomaly analysis → local fine-tuned Phi-3 answers in ~50 ms, cuts inference cost ~40%, and sensor data never leaves the plant. Proprietary trading rules → internally-deployed fine-tuned SLM with fully governed access.

31The pragmatic hierarchy: prebuilt copilot (80% fit) → SLM for the specialized edge case → custom large model only if neither works.
32The closing question of the whole Plan domain: "what's the simplest solution that solves the problem?"
🧠 Hook: scooter beats truck on C·P·L·S — Cost, Privacy, Latency, Specialization. If a scenario stresses any two of those plus high volume, the SLM answer is in play.
